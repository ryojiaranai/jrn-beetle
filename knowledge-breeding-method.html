Breeding Methods

How to maintain stable bloodlines with minimal inbreeding effects. Learn line breeding and round breeding — two proven methods used by professional beetle breeders.

1. Breeding Terminology

Before learning breeding methods, you need to understand these key terms:

Inbreeding
Mating a male and female that came from the same parents. Can stabilize traits but increases risk of genetic problems.
Crossbreeding
Mating a male and female from different bloodlines. Adds genetic diversity and hybrid vigor.
CB
Captive Bred — beetles bred in captivity (not from the wild).
WD
Wild Caught — beetles collected directly from the wild.
CBF1
Captive Bred F1 — first generation born in captivity. CBF2 = second generation, and so on.
WF1
Wild F1 — first generation offspring from wild-caught parents.
Important Rule — Generation Reset
When you crossbreed a WF or WD beetle with a different bloodline, the offspring resets to CBF1. This is because you've created a new mixed bloodline — a new captive-bred line starts from that point.

2. Line Breeding

Line breeding is a method to keep your bloodline stable while avoiding the problems of direct inbreeding (brother x sister). The key idea: start with one male and multiple females, then rotate males between groups every generation.

Step 1 — Start with 1 Male + 3 Females

Mate one male with 3 (or more) different females. This creates 3 separate groups of offspring that all share the same father.

Result: 3 groups of CBF1 offspring — Group A , Group B , Group C

Step 2 — Rotate Males Between Groups

In the next generation, pick a male from each group and mate it with a female from a different group. Follow this rotation pattern:

Rotation Pattern (Every Generation)
1 A Male × B Female → New Group A
2 B Male × C Female → New Group B
3 C Male × A Female → New Group C
🔄
Repeat this rotation every generation
A♂ × B♀  |  B♂ × C♀  |  C♂ × A♀
Why This Works

Since the groups are never directly inbred (brother x sister), the bloodline stays healthier. But because they all started from the same male, the traits stay stable. The blood slowly gets closer over generations, but much slower than direct inbreeding. Technically it stays CBF1 each time, but some breeders count CBF2+ when mating within related lines.

More Groups = Slower Blood Closing
Starting with more females (4, 5, or even 6) creates more groups, which means the blood takes even longer to become close. But it also means more work and more space. 3 groups is the standard starting point for most breeders.

3. Round Breeding

Round breeding is a more advanced method. It uses two original stable bloodlines (called Line A and Line B) and rotates crossbreeding with them across generations. This keeps the blood diverse while still developing the traits you want.

Requirement

Line A and Line B must be very stable bloodlines. You need to keep producing A and B throughout the entire process (using line breeding or buying from a breeder who maintains the same bloodline). They are your "foundation stock."

What's Happening

Groups C and D are "weighted" — one side of the bloodline is heavier (~70/30). The offspring won't look hugely different, but the traits you want from A or B will show up more. Group E keeps the balanced 50/50 mix for strain preservation. This also helps you learn which traits are dominant vs. recessive.

Generation 2 — Swap the Original Lines

Now comes the key move: swap A and B. Cross Group C with Line B, and Group D with Line A. This brings the bloodline weight back toward balance — but not exactly 50/50, so it avoids true inbreeding.

The Goal of Swapping
The closer the bloodline weight is to 50/50, the stronger the offspring (hybrid vigor). But you're NOT inbreeding because each generation adds fresh genetics from the original lines. The blood is related but far from close — that's the power of round breeding.

Generation 3 and Beyond — Freedom to Choose

By now you have many groups (C, D, E, F, G...) that are all related bloodlines but far from close blood. You have many options:

  • Cross any group with Line A or Line B to weight traits
  • Cross groups with each other (e.g., F × G) for balanced offspring
  • Keep line breeding Group E to preserve your developed strain
  • Test specific trait combinations by crossing selected groups
💡 Core Idea of Round Breeding
Always keep your original Lines A and B alive and producing.

Every generation, swap which original line you cross with:
If you used A last time → use B this time.
If you used B last time → use A this time.

This creates offspring that are always related but never too close — giving you stable traits with strong genetics.
Pro Tip

Round breeding also helps you learn about dominant and recessive genes in your beetles. By crossing weighted groups (70/30) with the opposite line, you can observe which traits persist and which disappear — revealing the genetic structure of your bloodline.

Line Breeding vs. Round Breeding

When to Use Each Method
Line Breeding — simpler, good for maintaining one bloodline. Best when you have a single high-quality line you want to keep pure. Requires less space and fewer beetles.

Round Breeding — more complex, but more powerful. Best when you want to develop new traits while keeping genetics strong. Requires two stable original lines and more groups. Used by advanced breeders developing rare color morphs.
