How to Keep Adult Beetles

Everything you need to know about housing, feeding, and caring for adult beetles — stag beetles, rhino beetles, and flower beetles. A complete beginner's guide.

Housing & Enclosure

Adult beetles need a simple setup — a container with substrate, something to climb on, and food. The container should be at least 3 times the beetle's body length in width, length, and height.

What You Need

  • Container — plastic box or glass tank with ventilation holes. Clear is better so you can observe.
  • Substrate — coconut fiber (coco coir), or a mix of coco coir and organic soil. 5-8 cm deep.
  • Climbing objects — bark, sticks, or small branches. Beetles can die if they flip over and can't right themselves.
  • Hide spots — pieces of bark or cork bark for beetles to hide under during the day.
  • Food dish — a small shallow dish or beetle jelly cup holder.
Tip

Always add climbing objects like sticks and bark. If a beetle flips on its back and can't grab anything to turn over, it will exhaust itself and die.

Temperature & Humidity

Most tropical beetle species (stag, rhino, flower) do best at room temperature. Avoid direct sunlight — beetles cannot regulate their body temperature and will overheat.

Temperature Guide by Type

Tropical Species (most stag & rhino beetles)
23-27 °C (73-80 °F). This includes rainbow stag beetles, Dynastes hercules, Chalcosoma, Japanese rhino beetles, and most flower beetles.
Temperate Species
18-22 °C (64-72 °F). European stag beetles (Lucanus cervus), some highland species. These prefer cooler conditions.

How to Maintain Humidity

Mist the substrate with a spray bottle every 1-2 days. The substrate should feel slightly damp but not wet or muddy. A simple test: squeeze a handful of substrate — if a few drops come out, the moisture is perfect. If water streams out, it's too wet.

Warning

Too wet = mold and bacteria growth, which can harm your beetle. Too dry = beetle dehydrates. Aim for "damp sponge" level moisture.

Feeding

Adult beetles eat fruit and tree sap in the wild. In captivity, the easiest and best option is beetle jelly — a specially formulated gel food that provides all the nutrition beetles need.

Best Foods

  • Beetle jelly (recommended) — balanced nutrition, long lasting, doesn't attract fruit flies
  • Banana — the most popular natural fruit for beetles, high energy
  • Apple — good alternative, lasts longer than banana
  • Grape — accepted by most species

Foods to Avoid

  • Watermelon — too watery, can make substrate too wet
  • Citrus fruits (orange, lemon) — most beetles reject these
  • Processed food — sugar, honey, or anything artificial
Tip

Replace fruit every 2-3 days to prevent mold and fruit flies. Beetle jelly cups can last 3-5 days. You do NOT need to provide water — beetles get all the moisture they need from food and substrate.

Daily Care Routine

  1. Check food — replace jelly or fruit if empty or moldy
  2. Mist substrate — if it looks dry, spray lightly
  3. Check beetle — make sure it's not stuck on its back
  4. Remove waste — clean up any mold spots or old food

That's it! Adult beetles are low-maintenance pets. Most of the time they rest during the day and become active at night.

Lifespan

Adult beetle lifespan varies by species. Once a beetle reaches adult form, it will not grow larger — its size is determined during the larval stage.

Important

Good food (protein beetle jelly), proper temperature, and correct humidity can significantly extend your beetle's lifespan. Stress, poor food, and temperature extremes shorten it.

Keeping Males & Females Together

In general, do not keep multiple males together — they will fight, especially stag beetles and rhino beetles. Males can injure or kill each other.

Stag Beetles
Keep males separate. Males fight aggressively with their mandibles and can seriously injure each other. One male + one female is okay temporarily for mating.
Rhino Beetles
Keep males separate. Males use their horns to flip rivals. One male + one female is fine for breeding.
Flower Beetles
Most flower beetles can be kept communally — multiple males and females in one large container. They are generally peaceful. This is one reason flower beetles are great for beginners.

Common Mistakes

  • No climbing objects — beetle flips over and dies
  • Direct sunlight — overheats and kills the beetle
  • Substrate too wet — causes mold, bacteria, and mites
  • Substrate too dry — beetle dehydrates
  • No ventilation — stale air causes health problems
  • Keeping aggressive males together — fights and injuries
  • Using pine or cedar substrate — toxic to beetles. Use coconut fiber, aspen, or flake soil only
